# Early Returns — Concept Validation
*March 2026*

---

## The Gap Is Real

The market has two distinct tiers with nothing in between.

**Tier 1 — Institutional analysis:** Cook Political Report, Inside Elections, Sabato's Crystal Ball. Narrative-driven, paywalled, written for political professionals. Authoritative but not data-transparent. You can't see the numbers behind the ratings.

**Tier 2 — Raw data:** FEC.gov, OpenSecrets, Congress.gov. Everything is there but requires significant effort to synthesize into a race-level picture. Researchers and journalists use it; engaged voters don't.

**Early Returns sits in an unclaimed middle:** synthesized, race-level, data-transparent, accessible to an intelligent non-professional reader.

---

## Differentiation

Nobody at the newsletter level is combining:
- Fundraising data (FEC)
- Voting behavior + party unity scores (Congress.gov)
- Race structure identification
- Ratings aggregation (Cook/Inside Elections/Sabato)

...into a single dashboard per race. That combination is the product.

---

## Feasibility

- FEC API and Congress.gov: free, well-documented, stable
- Data is there; automation is buildable
- Claude Code handles the data layer; human judgment handles curation and editorial

## What's Missing in the Marketplace
*Extensions to the core concept, ordered from closest to furthest*

### 1. Primary Tracking as Its Own Product Layer
A hefty number of House retirements in 2026 means primaries matter more than usual. Nobody is doing race-level primary dashboards for House races. Cook covers primaries narratively; nobody aggregates primary fundraising, candidate field composition, and ideological positioning into a dashboard. A gap right now, this cycle.

### 2. The "Who Flips" Tracker
Democrats need a net gain of just three seats to reach 218. A running tracker showing minimum flip scenarios — which specific combinations of races get Democrats to 218 — would be genuinely useful and shareable. Interactive, updated as ratings change. Nobody at this level is doing this visually.

### 3. Candidate Ideology Scoring Over Time
Party unity scores are a start. The extension is tracking *drift* — is this member moving toward or away from the party median over their career? A member who was a maverick in 2020 but voted with leadership 94% last year tells a different story than the reverse. Longitudinal data, visualized simply.

### 4. The "Local News Desert" Angle
Many competitive House districts have lost their local paper. No one is aggregating what little local coverage does exist alongside national coverage. Early Returns could be the only place doing race-level media monitoring for districts that national press ignores until October.

### 5. The AI-Transparent Political Dashboard as a Model
No one is publicly documenting how an AI-assisted political intelligence workflow is built — what it costs, where it breaks down, what human judgment is still required. That's a Gregory case study *and* a differentiating editorial angle. "Here's how this dashboard was built" as a recurring feature.

## The Honest Risk

Consistency. The product only works if it updates. A dashboard that goes stale loses credibility fast. The automation stack must carry the data layer so time goes to curation and editorial — not grinding through FEC filings.

---

## Strategic Positioning

- **This cycle (2026):** Build the engine. Develop familiarity with districts and data. Find the voice. Grow a small audience.
- **Next cycle (2028):** Show up with two cycles of credibility, a proven methodology, and a differentiated product. That's a real asset.
